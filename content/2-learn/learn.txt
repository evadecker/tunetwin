Title: Borrowing or Stealing?

----

Subtitle: How Copyright Law Shapes the Exchange of Musical Ideas

----

Text: 

On March 26, 2013, Pharrell Williams, Robin Thicke, and Clifford Harris, Jr. released the single “Blurred Lines”. Over the summer, it skyrocketed to the top of the charts, peaking at number one in 20 countries and becoming the first disco song to reach number one on Billboard since 1980. It was nominated for two Grammys and became one of the best-selling singles of all time. Yet its sweeping success attracted sharp criticism from the family of Marvin Gaye, who alleged that the single sounded too similar to Gaye’s 1977 hit “Got to Give It Up” and threatened to sue Thicke for copyright infringement. Pharrell, Thicke, and Harris took the offensive, preemptively suing the Gaye family and Bridgeport Music on August 15, 2013. Their lawsuit states,

> The basis of the Gaye defendants' claims is that "Blurred Lines" and "Got To Give It Up" "feel" or "sound" the same. Being reminiscent of a "sound" is not copyright infringement. The intent in producing "Blurred Lines" was to evoke an era. In reality, the Gaye defendants are claiming ownership of an entire genre, as opposed to a specific work, and Bridgeport is claiming the same work.

But in the eyes of the judiciary, Thicke had already taken a grave misstep. He told GQ in a May 2013 interview that “Got to Give It Up” was one of his favorite songs—and that it had directly inspired “Blurred Lines.” Predictably, the Gaye family cited this as an admission of guilt. By April 2014, Thicke had changed his tune. He claimed he was “high on Vicodin and alcohol” at the time of writing and that Pharrell “wrote almost every single part of the song.”
